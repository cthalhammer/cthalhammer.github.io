## Abstract

This work explores the fascinating interplay between edge and bulk localisation in non-reciprocal disordered systems. We analyze how disorder and non-reciprocity compete to determine the localization properties of waves, leading to rich phase diagrams with distinct regimes of edge-dominated and bulk-dominated behavior.

## Key Contributions

### Theoretical Framework
- Development of a comprehensive mathematical framework for analyzing non-reciprocal disordered systems
- Rigorous characterization of the competition between edge and bulk localization mechanisms
- Derivation of effective medium theories for complex non-reciprocal media

### Localization Phenomena
- **Edge Localization**: Analysis of topologically protected edge states in disordered non-reciprocal systems
- **Bulk Localization**: Investigation of Anderson localization in the presence of non-reciprocity
- **Competing Regimes**: Identification of parameter regimes where edge and bulk effects compete

### Mathematical Methods
- Spectral analysis techniques for non-Hermitian random matrices
- Asymptotic methods for studying localization transitions
- Numerical algorithms for computing localization lengths

## Physical Insights

### Non-Reciprocal Effects
The presence of non-reciprocity fundamentally alters the localization landscape:
- Breaks time-reversal symmetry, leading to complex eigenvalue spectra
- Creates directional wave propagation even in disordered media
- Enables novel topological phases absent in reciprocal systems

### Phase Diagrams
We establish comprehensive phase diagrams showing:
- **Edge-dominated regime**: Strong topological protection overcomes disorder
- **Bulk-dominated regime**: Anderson localization dominates over edge effects
- **Crossover regime**: Competing mechanisms with rich intermediate behavior

## Applications

### Metamaterials
- Design principles for non-reciprocal metamaterials with controlled localization
- Engineering disorder to enhance or suppress specific propagation modes
- Applications to waveguides and acoustic devices

### Condensed Matter Physics
- Understanding transport in non-reciprocal electronic systems
- Implications for quantum Hall effects in disordered samples
- Connections to active matter and driven systems

## Technical Approach

### Mathematical Formulation
We consider Hamiltonians of the form:
```
H = H₀ + V + Γ
```
where:
- H₀: Clean non-reciprocal Hamiltonian
- V: Random disorder potential
- Γ: Non-reciprocal coupling terms

### Analytical Methods
- **Green's function techniques**: For computing local density of states
- **Transfer matrix methods**: For studying localization lengths
- **Effective medium theory**: For homogenization in the weak disorder limit

### Numerical Simulations
- Large-scale numerical diagonalization of random matrices
- Monte Carlo averaging over disorder realizations
- Finite-size scaling analysis

## Results and Discussion

### Key Findings
1. **Critical Disorder Strength**: Identification of critical disorder strengths where edge-bulk competition is most pronounced
2. **Scaling Laws**: Universal scaling behavior near localization transitions
3. **Topological Protection**: Quantification of how disorder affects topological edge states

### Novel Phenomena
- **Hybrid Localization**: States that are simultaneously edge-like and bulk-like
- **Directional Localization**: Preferential localization along specific directions
- **Dynamic Crossover**: Time-dependent transitions between edge and bulk behavior

## Significance

This work advances our fundamental understanding of localization phenomena in complex systems where topology, disorder, and non-reciprocity compete. The results have implications for:

- **Basic Science**: New insights into localization theory and topological phases
- **Technology**: Design of robust non-reciprocal devices
- **Materials Science**: Understanding transport in complex media

The interplay between edge and bulk localization represents a new paradigm in wave physics, with potential applications ranging from photonics to condensed matter physics.
